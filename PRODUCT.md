# Product

## Register

product

## Users

ParkQuest serves families exploring parks together, beginning with families in Bellingham and designed to expand to other regions. Adults use it on mobile devices before, during, and after outings to discover parks, check amenities, record shared visits, and build a private family passport without creating child-specific profiles or exposing family activity publicly.

## Product Purpose

ParkQuest turns ordinary park visits into a repeatable family adventure. It combines a public park atlas with private family progress: anyone can find useful park information, while signed-in families can stamp visits, preserve memories, earn Adventure Points and stickers, and complete Daily Quests. Success means families can confidently choose a park, spend more time outside together, and build a meaningful record of their outings with minimal friction.

## Brand Personality

Outdoorsy, warm, and trustworthy. ParkQuest should feel playful enough to make exploration and rewards engaging without becoming childish. Public information should remain calm and legible; private passport and reward moments may carry more energy and delight.

## Anti-references

Avoid generic glassmorphism, excessive gradients, glowing effects, decorative visual noise, raw black debug-style surfaces, and unrelated palettes. Avoid making the family experience feel like a competitive social network, a children's cartoon, or a dense municipal data portal.

## Design Principles

1. Make the next family outing easy to choose and start.
2. Keep public park facts calm, clear, and trustworthy.
3. Reserve playfulness and celebration for meaningful progress moments.
4. Protect family privacy through both product behavior and interface language.
5. Design mobile-first for use around real-world outings, not only at a desk.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.2 Level AA. Support keyboard navigation, visible focus states, sufficient text and control contrast, semantic structure, color-independent state communication, appropriately sized touch targets, and reduced-motion preferences. Use plain, family-friendly language and avoid assumptions about family structure, ability, or familiarity with local parks.
